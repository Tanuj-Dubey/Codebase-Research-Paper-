Abstract
Addressing the need for multiple health indicators is critical to improving prediction 
accuracy and reducing the limitation of reliance on a single health indicator. This 
paper presents an AutoML model for accurately forecasting the life span of lithium-
ion batteries (LIBs) in electric vehicles. Unlike previous studies focusing solely on 
constant current (CC) and constant voltage (CV) charging, our AutoML approach 
includes ICA and direct analysis to generate eight novel multi-dimensional health 
indicators. These novel comprehensive indicators characterizing battery aging, 
including the position values of CC charging (IC_CC_P), the peak of CV charg-
ing (IC_CV_ H), and the peak/position of CC discharging (IC_D_H, IC_D_ P), 
are chosen based on correlation analysis and combined with four health indicators 
from direct analysis, i.e., capacity, cycle, cluster, and checkpoint, to improve predic-
tion accuracy. K-means clustering is employed to group similar data points. At the 
same time, the ruptures library facilitates the implementation of a novel algorithm 
for change point detection, allowing for the identification of checkpoints within 
each battery dataset. Experimental validation using NASA’s AMES LIB cycle life 
datasets demonstrates a significant performance improvement from existing recent 
methods, with 58.93 % lower mean absolute error (MAE) and 52.66 % lower root-
mean-square error (RMSE) compared to recent AutoML-based methods. This shows 
a significant improvement in forecasting the RUL of electric vehicle LIBs using the 
proposed AutoML-based approach with novel multi-dimensional health indicators.

1 Introduction
The significant impact of fossil fuel-based transport on air pollution and greenhouse 
gas emissions has been widely acknowledged [1, 2]. Advanced environmentally 
friendly technologies offer promising solutions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions 
and enhance air quality [3, 4]. Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have gained prominence 
in emerging technologies due to their impressive energy density and extended oper -
ational life [5–8]. LIBs are the preferred considerations for energy storage options 
in electric vehicles (EVs), and their secure use promotes secondary utilization and 
material recycling, thereby reducing the carbon footprint during production and 
recycling phases [9]. Consequently, the reliability and safety of LIBs have become 
areas of focus.
Nevertheless, it is crucial to recognize that the decline in the performance of 
LIBs is an inevitable process [10]. Traditionally, LIBs have reached the end of their 
operational life when their capacity drops to approximately 70 % of the initial value 
or when the internal resistance doubles. Even at this stage, LIBs can still find appli-
cations in various capacities, such as energy storage systems and electric bicycles 
[11]. However, unlike controlled laboratory conditions, real-world usage patterns 
may result in erratic battery performance and capacity degradation. This unpredicta-
bility can lead to inefficient battery cell usage, resource wastage, and potential safety 
hazards without precise RUL predictions. A comprehensive understanding of evolv-
ing degradation patterns in battery life span is crucial to avoiding premature replace-
ments and excessive usage. Consequently, predicting the life span and estimating the 
capacity of lithium-ion batteries present substantial challenges.
Predicting the RUL of LIBs involves both model-driven and data-driven 
approaches. In the model-driven system, intricate mathematical formulations are 
created by leveraging the inherent aging process of batteries. These models are 
carefully designed with mechanistic knowledge obtained through mathematical 
deduction. Techniques such as the expectation-maximization method [12] and the 
extended Kalman filtering method [13] are mathematical approaches to parameter -
izing these models. However, achieving precise forecasts is challenging due to the 
intricacy of the battery aging process and potential disparities between data disper -
sion and the assumptions of the underlying framework.
The data-driven technique has gained prominence due to its accessibility to data 
and advancements in machine learning techniques, including machine learning, arti-
ficial neural networks, and deep learning, for forecasting RUL. In data-driven RUL 
forecasting, two main approaches are typically followed: One directly utilizes the 
data for RUL predictions, while the other involves preprocessing the data to extract 
relevant health indicators. Subsequently, appropriate models are applied to these 
indicators for RUL prediction.
Various direct data-driven techniques are employed for forecasting the RUL 
of LIBs. These include traditional machine learning models such as GBDT [14], 
Gaussian regression [15], and random forest [16]. Additionally, deep learning 
techniques, including MLP [17], RNN [18], LSTM [8 ], Dual-LSTM [19], GRU 
[20], and Transformers [21], are widely utilized. These methods present various

advantages and trade-offs concerning computational efficiency and their capac-
ity to capture temporal information and long-range dependencies. Transformers, 
known for their effectiveness in encoding sequences, are gaining attention for 
their potential to enhance RUL prediction alongside traditional physical models.
Numerous research studies have focused on predicting batteries’ RUL through 
data-driven methods. These approaches involve extracting health indicators (HIs) 
from diverse sources and training predictive models using these indicators. The 
research encompasses a variety of health indicators and methodologies, includ-
ing input voltage, current, and temperature measurements, as well as factors such 
as incremental capacity curves, charge–discharge periods, isobaric voltage drop 
time, and more [15, 16, 22–28].
A data-driven RUL forecasting model using machine learning and deep learn-
ing is effective but demands expertise in setup and hyperparameter tuning [29]. 
Complex models with numerous hyperparameters require laborious effort. There 
is a growing need for models with adaptable structures to enhance data-driven 
exploration in RUL prediction and reduce human intervention. AutoML addresses 
this by automatically selecting models and adjusting hyperparameters based on 
data characteristics. In a recent study by Luo et al. [30], AutoML techniques [31] 
running in the AutoSklearn architecture were employed to predict the RUL of 
LIBs. Health indicators (HIs) were extracted through incremental capacity (IC) 
analysis, and a smoothing IC curve based on the Kalman filter algorithm was 
applied. Three HIs were identified as effective in characterizing the aging phe-
nomenon during the charging process, including the constant current (CC) and 
voltage (CV) phases.
However, the method outlined in [30] requires prolonged training time and 
concentrates solely on the CC–CV charge cycle, neglecting discharge cycle data 
in deriving health indicators. This limitation makes it less suitable for rapid and 
dynamic RUL prediction. Furthermore, the recent literature surveys suggest that the 
aging pattern of lithium batteries is strongly associated with the peaks observed in 
the IC curve during both charging and discharging cycles [32–34].
Additionally, features extracted from the CC discharge phase, such as voltage 
degradation rate and capacity fade, are critical for detecting signs of battery degra-
dation. Furthermore, changes in discharge curve characteristics over time can indi-
cate aging-related phenomena such as electrode degradation, electrolyte decompo-
sition, and impedance growth, all of which affect the battery’s RUL. Nonetheless, 
constant current discharge in lithium-ion battery analysis supports controlled experi-
ments, simplifies data collection, and improves replicability. Its simple voltage and 
current profiles make analysis easier, ensuring safety within specified limits while 
increasing efficiency in battery characterization and predictive modeling.
Thus, keeping the above in mind, to enhance RUL estimation, this paper intro-
duces AutoML experiments for predictions, incorporating novel multi-health indi-
cators from both the CC and CV charging phases, along with the CC discharging 
phase, with the intention of achieving higher accuracy. The significant contributions 
of this method include:

2  Related work
Employing a data-centric framework, we leverage historical data to forecast battery 
degradation patterns. The weight parameters in this framework are meticulously 
determined through comprehensive training data analysis, eliminating the need for 
reliance on physics-based techniques. The widespread acknowledgment of data-
driven approaches is attributed to their adaptability and extensive applicability on 
a global scale. Data-driven RUL prediction employs two primary strategies: The 
first directly utilizes raw data for RUL predictions, while the second involves an ini-
tial preprocessing step to extract essential health indicators. Subsequently, special-
ized models are applied to these indicators, ensuring accurate and effective RUL 
predictions.
Richardson et al. [35] proposed a Gaussian process regression algorithm for fore-
casting the RUL of LIBs, demonstrating outstanding long-term forecasting perfor -
mance. In another study by Yun et al. [36], a hybrid prognostic approach for RUL 
estimation in LIBs was introduced, integrating the gray model, autoregressive inte-
grated moving average, and variational mode decomposition models to form a com-
prehensive RUL prediction strategy. Additionally, Zhang et  al. [37] presented an 
innovative method for estimating the SOH and predicting RUL in LIBs, utilizing the 
IC curve during the constant current discharge phase and an ANN with four layers. 
Furthermore, Sun et al. [38] proposed a cloud-edge collaborative technique to esti-
mate the SOH in LIBs and optimize capacity prediction, incorporating a backpropa-
gation neural network enhanced by a genetic algorithm.
Researchers have recently delved into diverse deep learning methods for predict-
ing different systems’ RUL. Dong et al. [39] addressed the gradient explosion issue 
in RUL prediction by employing long short-term memory (LSTM). Zraibi et al. [40] 
introduced a comprehensive CNN-LSTM-DNN algorithm, leveraging each hybrid 
approach to enhance RUL prediction significantly. Wang and colleagues [41] pro-
posed an innovative mixed method for online life prediction, combining a bidirec-
tional LSTM with attention mechanism (BiLSTM-AM) model with support vector 
regression (SVR). This method involved gathering initial data, updating them with 
SVR, and utilizing BiLSTM-AM to estimate cycle life. In another approach, Tang 
[42] used a self-designed LSTM network named IRes2Net-BiGRU-FC and a deep 
neural network (DNN) to predict both high-frequency and low-frequency compo-
nents of the original data, demonstrating robust RUL prediction during both the CC 
and CV phases.
Data-driven RUL prediction based on HIs involves extracting these indicators and 
subsequent model training, with numerous scholars conducting extensive research 
in this domain. Ansari et al. [22] explored RUL prediction using health indicators. 
In contrast, Wang et al. [23] considered voltage, current, and temperature measure-
ments as HIs for RUL prediction, employing the gradient boosting decision tree. 
Meng et al. [15] and Wang et al. [24] adopted a multi-HI approach, extracting data

language processing [47], stock forecasting [46, 48], COVID-19 pandemic analy -

simplifies model selection and hyperparameter tuning. Although successful, the 
use of AutoML for forecasting RUL remains in its early stages and poses unique 
challenges.
In recent work, AutoML22 [30], an AutoML model based on AutoSklearn archi-
tecture, has been applied to predict the RUL of LIBs by extracting HIs through 
incremental capacity (IC) analysis. The authors comprehensively analyzed the CC 
and CV phases, emphasizing the strong correlation between the IC curve during the 
CC phase and the charging capacity in the CV phase and their impact on battery life 
span. Furthermore, estimating lithium-ion battery SOH or RUL through ICA has 
gained significant attention. Researchers in [53, 54] have demonstrated a strong cor-
relation between the peak position and intensity of the IC curve during constant cur-
rent charging processes and the deterioration of LIB’s State Of Health (SOH). Addi-
tionally, the aging pattern of lithium batteries is closely associated with the peaks 
observed in the IC curve during both charging and discharging cycles [32–34].
Considering the significant associations observed with health indicators derived 
from the discharge cycle IC curve in tracking capacity changes and the practical 
importance of constant current discharge cycles, which facilitate controlled experi-
ments, reduce external factors, and ensure consistent data collection, thereby 
enhancing the efficiency and reliability of lithium-ion battery characterization and 
predictive modeling endeavors. Moreover, recognizing the importance of an auto-
mated machine learning model in streamlining model selection, hyperparameter 
optimization, and feature selection processes, along with incorporating multiple 
health indicators for improved prediction accuracy and addressing the limitations

3  Background
This section outlines the problem formulation and introduces the AutoML frame-
works (models) employed for our analysis.
3.1  Problem formulation
Accurate prediction of RUL in electric vehicle LIBs is critical for ensuring EV bat-
tery system reliability. The Prognostics and Health Management (PHM) discipline 
emphasizes the importance of RUL in ensuring device safety, preventing unexpected 
failures, informing maintenance decisions, and conducting SOH assessments.
The State Of Health (SOH), a crucial health indicator for battery aging, reflects 
the battery’s condition throughout each charge–discharge cycle. SOH plays a sig-
nificant role in assessing the extent of battery degradation, often quantified by the 
capacity ratio [55].
Here, Cr represents the rated capacity, while C t denotes the measured capacity for 
cycle t.
Moreover, battery End-Of-Life (EOL), closely tied to its capacity, is the point at 
which the remaining capacity reaches 70–80 % of the initial capacity, corresponding 
to a SOH of 0.7–0.8. Consequently, RUL signifies the available service time (num-
ber of charge and discharge cycles) remaining before the battery’s capacity deterio-
rates to the failure threshold of 70-80 % of its initial capacity [21].
This paper used capacity prediction to indirectly forecast RUL by leveraging the 
previously established relationship between SOH and RUL. This approach is con-
sistent with methodologies reported in the existing data-driven RUL forecasting lit-
erature, as cited in related works.

3.2.1  Model selection
AutoML’s primary aim is to autonomously identify the most accurate machine 
learning framework from a given set for a specific dataset. To accomplish this, 
AutoML typically employs a technique called algorithm selection or meta-learning. 
This involves evaluating various machine learning algorithms, such as decision 
trees, random forests, support vector machines, and neural networks, and selecting 
the one that demonstrates the best performance on the dataset. AutoML frameworks 
often include libraries of preimplemented algorithms, enabling them to systemati-
cally explore various models and configurations efficiently [60].
3.2.2  Hyperparameter optimization (HPO)
Hyperparameters are parameters that control the learning process and affect the 
performance of a machine learning model. Examples include the number of hidden 
layers, batch size, activation function, optimizer, and learning rate. Hyperparameter 
optimization, known as hyperparameter tuning, involves searching for the best com-
bination of hyperparameters to maximize the model’s performance on a validation 
dataset. Hyperparameter optimization is crucial in machine learning, especially with 
the scaling of neural networks to improve precision. Traditional optimization relies

3.2.3  Feature selection
AutoML incorporates automated feature engineering to streamline the manual and 
repetitive tasks involved in this process. Feature engineering entails creating new 
features or transforming existing ones to enhance the performance of machine learn-
ing models. This can include scaling, normalization, encoding categorical variables, 
generating interaction terms, and utilizing dimensionality reduction techniques like 
principal component analysis.
AutoML frameworks often integrate automated feature engineering capabilities 
that preprocess the data, select relevant features, and generate new ones to improve 
model performance. These techniques can be applied individually or in combination 
with other feature selection and extraction methods to optimize predictive power. 
Various systems, such as ExploreKit, Data Science Machine, and Cognito, have 
been developed for automatic feature engineering. ExploreKit ranks features using

employs profound feature synthesis for relational data. Cognito utilizes a hierarchi-
cal approach to feature construction, enhancing precision through a greedy strat-
egy. These systems streamline and automate the feature engineering process within 
AutoML.
4  Methodology
This section covers the architecture of the suggested method in detail, describes 
the dataset used and how it was visualized, and explains how multi-health indica-
tors were extracted. Next, we will demonstrate AutoML-based models, including 
AutoTS, TPOT, H2O, and AutoKeras. We also present the findings from the correla-
tion analysis results alongside the model training process in three scenarios: indi-
vidual battery, 2P1, and leave-one-out.
4.1  Architecture
Accurately estimating RUL through historical data necessitates using an optimal 
feature extraction technique that effectively captures features strongly correlated 
with RUL. The proposed method prioritizes enhancing RUL prediction accuracy by 
extracting critical health indicators associated with the aging of LIBs.
The proposed work utilized a dataset from the NASA AMES Prognostics Center 
focusing on the aging of 18,650 lithium-ion batteries, specifically analyzing four 
 S. Mishra et al.
1 3
batteries labeled B005, B006, B007, and B0018. The methodology involved lev -
eraging multiple health indicators (HIs) obtained through ICA and direct analysis. 
The IC curve was initially computed during the constant current (CC) and constant 
voltage (CV) charging phases, extracting two HIs: maximum point height and posi-
tion. Subsequently, another IC curve was generated for the CC discharge phase, 
extracting two additional HIs: maximum point height and position. Only four of the 
six obtained HIs were selected following widely used Pearson and Spearman cor -
relation analyses. These included the position values of CC charging (IC_CC_P), 
peak of CV charging (IC_CV_H), and peak/position of CC discharging (IC_D_H, 
IC_D_P). These four HIs, combined with the four HIs acquired from direct analysis, 
i.e., capacity, cycle, cluster, and checkpoint, resulted in eight HIs. Collectively, these 
eight health indicators (HIs) comprehensively characterize the battery’s aging pro-
cess during both the charging and discharging phases. Moreover, the AutoML model 
utilizes these eight HIs to predict the RUL.
The proposed methodology is visually depicted in Fig.  2, providing a clear over-
view of the approach. The architecture presented in Fig.  2, portraying the proposed 
model, is primarily composed of four main sections:
• Extraction of HIs (IC_CC_P and IC_CV_H) from charge cycles through con-
stant current and constant voltage data extraction.
• Extraction of HIs (IC_D_H and IC_D_P) from discharge cycles using constant 
voltage discharge cycle data.
• Extraction of HIs (capacity, cycle, cluster, and checkpoint) from direct analysis.
• Utilization of an AutoML model for RUL prediction.
These obtained HIs are partitioned into training and testing datasets. The training 
data are then utilized to instruct an AutoML model (H2O), with capacity as the tar -
get variable. Subsequently, the model is applied to predict capacity, and its accuracy 
and efficiency are evaluated using the detailed evaluation method discussed in sub-
sequent sections.

Qc signifies the battery’s charging capacity, while Vc represents its terminal voltage

capacity, and Vd corresponds to its terminal voltage during the discharging process. 
Furthermore, we have ic , which denotes the charging current, and id , representing the

The IC curves method serves as an effective means of translating the character -
istic voltage plateaus in a battery’s behavior into discernible variations on the IC 
curve. These variations are crucial in accurately detecting subtle changes as the bat-
tery undergoes aging [63, 64]. Notably, the aging process, particularly during charg-
ing, influences the extent of reactions between the battery’s electrodes. While these 
variations might not be immediately apparent in the voltage curve, they manifest as 
inflection points or peaks in the IC curve [64]. This characteristic allows for valua-
ble monitoring of the battery’s condition and potentially forecasting its aging trajec-
tory. As the capacity gradually degrades due to aging, these changes become quan-
titatively identifiable within the IC curve, rendering it a powerful tool for assessing 
battery health and performance.
Consequently, in the scope of our analysis, the position and height of the peak 
within the CC charging and CV charging IC curves, respectively, form the founda-
tion for deriving two health indices. Likewise, the height and position of the peak 
within the discharging IC curve are utilized to compute our other two health indices. 
Figures 7 and 8 visually present the pivotal IC curves for each battery during CC 
charge and CC discharge cycles, respectively. These figures offer essential insights 
for our analysis, highlighting the distinctive features and variations in the IC curves 
across different charging and discharging conditions.
Furthermore, plotting the IC curve can result in a distorted transmission of char -
acteristics during the CV charging phase. Similar to recent works, AutoML22 [30], 
the CV phase’s HIs can be directly extracted without needing conversion through 
the IC method to ensure undistorted transmission of characteristics.

5.4  Discussion
The result analysis discussed above highlights the superior performance of the H2O 
AutoML model compared to existing models [8, 17–21, 30, 76–78], as well as the 
AutoML models employed in this paper, namely AutoKeras [74], TPOT [72, 73], 
and AutoTS [70], for predicting RUL in EV LIBs.
In this subsection, we analyze and compare the predictive performance of the 
H2O model in estimating RUL. The evaluation considers scenarios with and without 
checkpoints and cluster HIs across the three cases. Tables 18, 19, and 20 present the 
performance measures under both conditions across the three cases.
From Tables 18, 19, and 20, it becomes evident that in Cases-1 and 3, H2O mod-
els utilizing cluster and checkpoint HIs perform well for batteries B005 and B018, 
whereas the H2O model without using cluster and checkpoint HIs performs bet-
ter for B006 and B007. Furthermore, in Case-2, the MAE for all batteries is lower 
with H2O models without cluster and checkpoint HIs; however, the RMSE is lower, 
and the R-square is close to 1 with H2O models using cluster and checkpoint HIs.

tuning, slow training speeds, and limited effectiveness with small-scale data still 
need to be addressed. To address these challenges, we have proposed an AutoML-
based approach that uses four AutoML frameworks—AutoKeras, TPOT, H2O, 
and AutoTS on the NASA AMES Prognostics Center lithium-ion battery aging 
dataset. We examined specific batteries (B005, B006, B007, and B0018) and 
identified novel health indicators using incremental capacity analysis and direct 
analysis techniques. These indicators, such as CC charging position (IC_CC_P), 
CV charging peak (IC_CV_ H), and CC discharging peak/position (IC_D_H, 
IC_D_P), capacity, cycle, cluster, and checkpoint, were selected based on Pearson 
and Spearman correlation analyses to characterize battery aging. Furthermore, 
a comparative evaluation of the proposed innovative HIs-based AutoML model 
(H2O) on NASA’s LIBs battery dataset demonstrates superior performance 
across individual batteries, 2P1, and leave-one-out scenarios. The proposed meth-
ods outperform recent AutoML-based research, with a 58.93 % lower MAE and 
52.66 % lower RMSE.
Our next objective is to advance this research by determining model param-
eters for dynamic driving scenarios. We will validate our proposed model by sim-
ulating typical urban driving conditions, utilizing dynamic profiles such as the 
Urban Dynamometer Driving Schedule (UDDS). This will enable us to refine our 
predictions further and ensure the reliability of electric vehicles under real-world 
usage scenarios.
